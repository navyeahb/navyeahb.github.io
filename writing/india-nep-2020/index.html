India's Education Revolution: What Punctuated Equilibrium Theory Tells Us About the NEP 2020

PolicyJune 2026 ·

India has 1.5 billion people and still hasn't hit the global average for adult literacy. So when the government overhauled its entire education system in 2020 for the first time in 34 years, it was a big deal.

My final assessment in an elective course - Understanding Public Policy - asked me to explain a policy development through a theoretical lens. I chose to analyse India's National Education Policy (2020) using Punctuated Equilibrium Theory (PET), which argues that policy doesn't change gradually. It stays stuck, accumulates pressure, and then shifts all at once. I also explored how pairing PET with the Advocacy Coalition Framework (ACF) gives a more complete picture - capturing not just the when of policy change, but the why. Here's a brief synthesis of what I learnt:

  • The PET framework divides the policy process into sustained periods of stability - equilibria - that are interrupted by periods of rapid change - punctuation.
    • The 34 years between the NPE 1986 and NEP 2020 look like a textbook equilibrium. Nothing fundamental changed, even as the problems kept compounding:
      1. policy continuity - successive governments kept targeting the same unresolved goals of equity and access
      2. accumulating friction - teachers had little autonomy, and entrenched social hierarchies made reform politically difficult
      3. dominant policy monopoly - a single centralised body controlled school education with little challenge to its authority
    • The NEP 2020 then broke that equilibrium in one go. Three things mark it as a punctuation rather than just another incremental update:
      1. paradigm shift - scrapping the 10+2 school structure, moving from STEM to STEAM, and pushing universities to become truly multidisciplinary
      2. disproportionate overcorrection - abrupt moves like eliminating the M.Phil degree and creating an entirely new national assessment body (PARAKH)
      3. dismantling of monopolies - replacing a fragmented web of regulators with one unified body, the HECI
  • PET is powerful but it only tells you when change happened, not why. The Advocacy Coalition Framework fills that gap by looking at the belief systems and coalitions of actors driving policy. Using both together lets us capture the full picture - even beyond education policy in India.
